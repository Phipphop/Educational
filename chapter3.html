The Invisible Cost of Fuel

Before gasoline reaches your tank, it's already consumed enormous amounts of energy — on rigs, in refineries, in pipelines, and on tankers. That cost never shows up on the pump.

↓ scroll to find out

A Price You're Already Paying

In Chapter 2, we saw that a gasoline engine converts only about 20% of fuel energy into motion. But that's the optimistic version of the story — it starts measuring at the pump, as if the gasoline just appeared there for free.

It didn't. Getting a gallon of gasoline to your tank requires drilling wells, running enormous pumps, shipping crude oil across the ocean, feeding it through one of the most energy-intensive industrial processes on earth, and then trucking the finished product to tens of thousands of stations across the country.

Every one of those steps burns energy. None of it shows up in your mpg rating.

The Journey of a Gallon

Here's what happens before the gasoline hits your tank — and what it costs at each step.

🛢️

1. Extraction — The Well

Pumping oil from the ground requires energy: electric motors running pumpjacks, high-pressure water injection to push oil to the surface, natural gas burned on-site for heat and power. US onshore oil production alone consumes tens of billions of kWh per year in electricity. Modern shale oil requires significantly more energy per barrel than conventional oil from decades past — the easy oil is mostly gone.

6–12%
energy lost
🚢

2. Transport — Pipelines & Tankers

About half of US crude oil is imported. Moving it requires supertankers that burn heavy bunker fuel — one of the dirtiest fuels on earth — for weeks at sea. Domestically, crude and refined products travel thousands of miles through pipelines that require pumping stations, then load onto trucks for final-mile delivery to stations. This transport leg is a smaller share of the total, but it's energy burned for no one's benefit except logistics.

~2%
energy lost
🏭

3. Refining — The Hidden Factory

This is the big one. US oil refineries collectively process about 17–18 million barrels of crude every single day. Refining is an energy-intensive industrial process: cracking heavy hydrocarbons into lighter fuels requires extreme heat, pressure, and catalytic reactions running continuously. US refineries are among the largest industrial energy consumers in the country, operating 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

The energy to run these facilities has to come from somewhere — and much of it comes from burning a portion of the crude itself, or from purchased natural gas and electricity. Roughly one in every seven to ten barrels processed goes into powering the refinery that processes the other six to nine.

~12%
energy lost
⛽

4. The Pump — What You're Actually Buying

After extraction, transport, and refining, the fuel that reaches your tank contains roughly 75–80% of the original barrel's energy content. The rest has already been spent getting it to you.

~78%
remaining

5. The Engine — The Final Inefficiency

Then the 78% that made it to your tank gets burned in an engine that converts roughly 20% of it into motion. The other 80% leaves through the tailpipe and the radiator as heat.

78% × 20% = ~15.6% of the original barrel's energy actually moves your car.

The EV Shortcut

An electric vehicle skips almost the entire supply chain.

Electricity can be generated right where it's needed — on a rooftop, in a field, offshore — and transmitted directly to a car. There's no refinery. No tanker. No pipeline. No truck delivery to a station. The energy goes from generation to wheels in one short hop, with losses only in the wire and the motor.

The punchline

An EV on renewable electricity delivers 5–7 times more of the original energy source to the wheels than a gas car — counting every step from the primary source all the way through. The supply chain inefficiency isn't a minor footnote. It roughly doubles the gap we saw in Chapter 2.

The Shrinking Return on Oil

There's a concept called EROI — Energy Return on Energy Invested. It measures how much usable energy you get back for every unit of energy you put into extraction.

In the early 20th century, US oil had an EROI of roughly 100:1 — you invested 1 barrel of energy and got 100 back. Those were the easy fields, the shallow wells, the gushers. That oil is long gone.

Today, US shale oil — the source of America's recent production boom — has an EROI of roughly 8:1 to 15:1. You invest 1 unit of energy to extract 8–15. That sounds reasonable until you remember that refining, transport, and end-use inefficiency eat into the remainder. By the time that energy moves a car, you've extracted 15 but only used 2.

The trend is moving in the wrong direction. As easy-to-reach reserves deplete, EROI continues to decline — meaning we burn more and more energy just to get the energy we use.

What this means long-term

As oil EROI continues to decline, we burn more and more energy just to maintain current production levels. Solar and wind, by contrast, have EROI that is already competitive with or exceeding today's oil — and it improves as manufacturing scales. The economics of oil get worse over time. The economics of renewables get better.

Infrastructure Already Paying the Bill

The US oil industry runs on enormous amounts of electricity — electricity that the industry itself often frames as a necessary cost of keeping the fuel supply running. But look at the scale of that consumption from a different angle.

US onshore oil extraction uses electricity to run an estimated 350,000–450,000 pumpjacks, along with water injection systems, gas compression, and processing equipment. Estimates of this consumption suggest the electricity alone could power roughly 8–15 million electric vehicles for a year — before counting refinery consumption, pipeline pumping stations, or terminal operations.

US oil refineries collectively process 17–18 million barrels per day and are among the most energy-intensive industries in the country. A meaningful fraction of the crude they receive never leaves as refined product — it's consumed powering the refinery that processes the rest.

The key insight

We are spending electricity, natural gas, and diesel — right now, today — to deliver a fuel that will then be burned at 20% efficiency. An EV plugged into that same electricity skips the entire middle of that chain. The infrastructure cost doesn't go away by choosing gas. You're paying it either way. With an EV, you skip the inefficient end of the chain too.

A note on these numbers

Precise, audited electricity-consumption figures for the full US oil supply chain are not publicly published in a single place. The estimates above for pumpjack electricity are derived from industry surveys and are in the right order of magnitude but should be treated as indicative, not precise. The EROI figures are well-sourced from peer-reviewed literature. We flag this rather than obscure it.

Common Objections

"We still need oil for plastics, chemicals, and aviation — this doesn't argue for eliminating oil."
+

Correct — and this series doesn't argue for eliminating oil. About 70% of US petroleum goes to transportation fuels (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel). Plastics, chemicals, lubricants, and other non-fuel uses make up the remaining 30% and have no near-term electric substitute at scale.

The argument here is narrower: for the transportation fuel use case specifically, electricity is dramatically more efficient from source to wheels, and the supply chain overhead for petroleum transportation fuel is a large hidden cost. Reducing petroleum's role in transportation doesn't require eliminating it everywhere.

"The electricity grid has its own supply chain losses — transmission, generation, etc."
+

Yes, and those are included in the well-to-wheel figures shown above. US grid transmission and distribution losses average about 5–6%. Generation losses depend on the source: natural gas combined cycle runs at ~49% efficiency; renewables have near-zero conversion losses.

Even accounting for all grid losses, the EV pathway from primary source to wheels is 4–7 times more efficient than the gasoline pathway — because the gasoline pathway stacks extraction, refining, transport, and engine inefficiency on top of each other. Each loss multiplies the previous ones. The EV pathway has far fewer multiplied losses.

"EROI for renewables seems suspiciously high. Is that accurate?"
+

EROI numbers for renewables vary significantly by study methodology, location, and system boundary. Some studies focused on grid-level integration (including storage and backup) produce lower EROI figures for renewables; others measuring only the panel/turbine itself produce higher ones. The figures shown (solar ~30:1, wind ~40:1) are from harmonized meta-analyses of peer-reviewed literature and are considered conservative mid-range estimates.

The key comparison isn't an exact ratio — it's the direction of the trend. Oil EROI is declining as reserves become harder to access. Renewable EROI is improving as manufacturing scales and technology matures. The crossover is not theoretical — it's already happened for solar and wind against unconventional oil sources like tar sands.

"What about hydrogen as a fuel? Couldn't that avoid these supply chain losses?"
+

Hydrogen faces its own efficiency chain problem, though it's better than petroleum for transportation. Electrolysis (splitting water with electricity) is about 60–80% efficient. Compressing or liquefying hydrogen loses another 10–15%. A fuel cell converts hydrogen back to electricity at about 60% efficiency. Combined: roughly 35–45% of the original electricity reaches the wheels.

Compare that to a battery EV, which retains about 77–85% of electricity at the wheels. Hydrogen fuel cell vehicles are about 2–2.5× less efficient than battery EVs on a well-to-wheel basis. Hydrogen does have compelling use cases where batteries don't work well — heavy freight, aviation, shipping, industrial processes. For light-duty passenger vehicles, battery EVs are significantly more efficient.
