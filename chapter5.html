Chapter 5

Sunshine on Everything

The solar debate assumes we need to claim new land. We don't. The surfaces we already use — rooftops, parking lots, farmland — could supply the majority of US electricity without displacing a single acre.

↓ scroll to find out

The Wrong Question

Critics of solar often frame the land use question like this: "You'd need to cover enormous areas of land to power the whole country — solar can't scale without destroying natural habitats."

That framing starts from a flawed premise. It imagines solar as something that goes instead of existing land uses — replacing farmland, forests, or open space. But solar panels don't care what's underneath them. They can sit on top of rooftops, float over parking spaces, and shade crops that grow better in partial shadow.

The question isn't where do we put solar. It's what are we already covering that could also be generating electricity?

Where Solar Actually Goes

🏠

Residential Rooftops Sourced

NREL's landmark 2016 study found that US residential rooftops alone could supply 926 TWh/year — about 22% of total US electricity demand. Add commercial buildings and the total reaches 1,432 TWh (34% of demand). These panels sit on surfaces that already exist, shelter buildings from heat (reducing cooling costs), and move electricity generation to where it's consumed — skipping most of the grid's 5–6% transmission losses. Roughly 3.3 million homes per year are built or re-roofed, each one an opportunity.

🚗

Parking Canopies Estimated

The United States has an estimated 500–800 million parking spaces — an astonishing amount of paved, sun-exposed surface generating nothing. Solar canopies over parking lots provide shade for cars (reducing interior temperatures and AC demand), generate power for EV charging directly on-site, and require no new land. If just 10% of US parking spaces were covered, the resulting generation would roughly equal the output of 40 large nuclear plants. France passed a law in 2023 requiring solar canopies on all parking lots with more than 80 spaces.

🌾

Agrivoltaics — Solar + Farming Sourced

Agrivoltaics — the practice of growing crops under or between solar panels — has moved from experiment to established practice. Research shows up to 186% combined land productivity in agrivoltaic systems compared to using the land for either solar or farming alone. The logic: partial shade from panels reduces water evaporation, extends the growing season for cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, herbs), and protects against extreme heat. Farmers earn lease revenue from solar developers while continuing to farm the same land. Studies from the DOE and several universities show the benefits are real across a wide range of crop types and climates.

🏭

Warehouses & Industrial Rooftops Sourced

Large commercial and industrial buildings — warehouses, distribution centers, manufacturing plants — often have large, flat, unshaded rooftops with structural capacity to support solar. Amazon, Walmart, Apple, and Target collectively operate some of the largest corporate solar installations in the world. The NREL study's 506 TWh/year from commercial rooftops is likely an underestimate, as it used 2014 solar efficiency figures; modern panels generate 20–30% more power from the same area. These installations generate electricity directly where energy-intensive operations need it, often in industrial zones far from residential areas.

💧

Reservoirs & Canals Estimated

Floating solar on water reservoirs and solar panels covering irrigation canals are both real and growing. California's Project Nexus showed that covering irrigation canals with solar panels reduced water evaporation by 39% while generating enough electricity to power 75,000 homes — from a stretch of canal that was already built and maintained. The US has roughly 40,000 miles of irrigation canals. Covering even a fraction would generate significant power while reducing water loss in drought-stressed regions — a dual benefit that costs less than building solar on land.

Close to Home Changes Everything

Utility-scale solar built in the desert is valuable. But solar on your roof does something different: it removes your home from the grid's demand side during the hours when the sun shines.

This is a different kind of benefit than adding supply at a central plant. Distributed generation:

→

Reduces transmission losses — electricity used where it's generated doesn't travel through hundreds of miles of wire, skipping the grid's ~5–6% transmission loss entirely.

→

Reduces peak demand — solar generation peaks in the afternoon when air conditioning demand peaks. Rooftop solar directly offsets the load that causes peaker plants to fire up (Chapter 4).

→

Reduces grid infrastructure strain — fewer electrons traveling long distances means less stress on substations, transformers, and distribution lines — extending their lifespans and reducing blackout risk.

→

Creates resilience — a home with solar and a battery can keep running during grid outages. A neighborhood with high solar penetration is partially insulated from regional supply disruptions.

Already at scale

In Hawaii, where electricity prices have historically been very high, over 30% of single-family homes now have rooftop solar. In California, new residential construction is required to include solar. This isn't a future scenario — it's a policy direction that's already in motion, and the economics support it even without mandates in most sunny states.

"But I Don't Live in the Sun Belt"

Solar is often dismissed as a technology that only works in Arizona. The reality is more nuanced — and more optimistic.

High solar

Southwest US

6–7 peak sun hours/day. Solar panels here produce 50–70% more electricity than the US average. The Mojave and Sonoran deserts are among the best solar resources on earth.

Good solar

Southeast & Plains

4.5–6 peak sun hours/day. Florida, Texas, and the Great Plains have excellent solar resources that are largely untapped. Florida generates less solar than Massachusetts despite far more sun.

Viable solar

Midwest & Northeast

3.5–4.5 peak sun hours/day. Germany — cloudier than any US state — generates ~12% of its electricity from solar and has 60 GW installed. The Northeast is economically viable, especially with high grid prices.

Still works

Pacific Northwest

3–4 peak sun hours/day. Lowest US solar resource, but still produces meaningful power in summer months. Often paired with abundant hydro and wind for a complementary mix.

The Germany comparison

Germany sits at roughly the latitude of Canada's Hudson Bay and has a cloudier climate than any US state. It has still deployed over 80 GW of solar capacity and generates ~12% of its total electricity from solar, with ambitions to reach 50% by 2035. The US is the Saudi Arabia of solar resources by comparison — and has deployed a fraction of what the economics justify.

Common Objections

"Solar panels degrade and wear out — isn't that a long-term problem?"
+

Solar panels do degrade over time, at a rate of about 0.5% per year — meaning a panel installed today will still produce about 87% of its original output after 25 years. Most manufacturers offer 25-year performance warranties. Modern panels tested in harsh climates have shown degradation rates even lower than this.

End-of-life is a legitimate question. Panel recycling infrastructure is in early stages — most panels today end up in landfills at end-of-life, which is a real problem the industry needs to solve. Several manufacturers now offer take-back programs, and the EU has mandatory recycling requirements. The issue is manageable and being actively worked on, but worth naming rather than dismissing.

"Homeowners and farmers can't afford solar — this only benefits the wealthy."
+

Cost access is a genuine equity issue. The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) covers 30% of solar installation costs, but only for homeowners who owe federal taxes — renters and low-income households often don't benefit. Community solar programs — where subscribers buy a share of a larger solar installation and receive credits on their utility bill — are expanding rapidly and provide solar benefits to renters and those without suitable rooftops.

For farmers, the economics of agrivoltaics are increasingly compelling: lease revenue from solar developers provides a stable income stream alongside crops, and the DOE and USDA have active programs supporting agrivoltaic adoption. The Rural Energy for America Program (REAP) provides grants and loans for agricultural solar installations.

"If everyone has rooftop solar, how does the utility make money and maintain the grid?"
+

This is a real regulatory challenge, and utilities are right to raise it. If customers generate their own power and the grid-fixed-cost recovery model doesn't adapt, the remaining non-solar customers end up paying more to maintain infrastructure — a "death spiral" dynamic that several utilities have experienced in high-solar markets.

The solution isn't to restrict rooftop solar — it's to restructure utility rate designs so grid maintenance costs are recovered through connection fees rather than per-kWh charges, and to expand utility business models into services (grid management, storage, EV charging). California, Hawaii, and several other states are actively working through these redesigns. It's a policy challenge, not a technical barrier to rooftop solar.

"Doesn't solar require rare or toxic materials that are hard to mine?"
+

The vast majority of solar panels (over 90%) use silicon — the second most abundant element in Earth's crust — as the primary semiconductor material. There's no scarcity issue with silicon. Some older thin-film technologies used cadmium telluride or copper indium gallium selenide, but silicon remains dominant.

Silver is used in solar panel electrodes and is the main material supply concern — panels use roughly 20 mg of silver per watt. As solar scales dramatically, this could create silver supply pressure. The industry is actively developing lower-silver and silver-free electrode technologies; silver content per panel has already dropped 90% over the past decade and continues to fall. Unlike lithium in batteries, silver isn't consumed during solar operation — it can be fully recovered at end-of-life.
