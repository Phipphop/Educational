A Buyer's Field Guide

Everything Worth Knowing About Owning a Tesla

The safety, the running costs, and the dozens of details that add up — laid out by the numbers, with every figure sourced and the caveats left in.

↓ start with safety

This guide centers on the Tesla Model Y — the best-selling vehicle in the world — and notes where the Model 3 meaningfully differs, usually on price or straight-line speed. It's written for someone who doesn't already know much about Teslas and wants the real case, not a sales pitch.

Every headline figure links to a primary source — Tesla's safety reports, NHTSA, IIHS, Euro NCAP, the EPA, the EIA. Where a popular claim doesn't survive scrutiny, it's been corrected or year-stamped rather than repeated. Prices and software move month to month; figures here are current to mid-2026.

01 — Safety

Two kinds of safety: avoiding the crash, and surviving it

A Tesla works to keep its occupants out of a collision in the first place — and then, if one is unavoidable, to protect them as well as anything on the road. Both halves are backed by data from independent testing bodies, not just the manufacturer.

Before the crash — the collisions that never happen

Tesla's driver-assistance software has now driven more than 10 billion miles with Full Self-Driving (Supervised) engaged, which makes its crash statistics unusually large-scale. The headline number is striking — but how it's framed matters enormously, so here are three ways to look at the same data, from the most favorable to the most conservative:

The big number: with FSD (Supervised) engaged, Tesla reports roughly one major collision every 5+ million miles, versus about 660,000 miles for the average US vehicle — on the order of 8× fewer crashes per mile. (Tesla Full Self-Driving Vehicle Safety Report, 2026.)

The honest read is the middle one: compared against the same Tesla drivers when they switch the software off, FSD shows about 7× fewer collisions overall and ~5× fewer on non-highway roads — which matters because it rebuts the most common objection, that the numbers only look good because assisted miles are easy highway miles. And an independent regulator (the Netherlands' RDW, which required the reporting as a condition of approval) found a similar direction: ~3.5× fewer collisions overall.

What's standard vs. what's paid

Every Tesla includes a full active-safety suite at no cost — automatic emergency braking, forward-collision warning, lane keeping, and blind-spot warning — plus standard Autopilot (traffic-aware cruise and lane centering). Full Self-Driving (Supervised) is a separate paid option, and despite the name it is a Level 2 system that requires an attentive driver. The safety case above rests on assisted driving, not autonomy.

The Model 3 earns the same 5-star ratings and was named Euro NCAP Best in Class (Large Family Car) for 2025; its IIHS award is Top Safety Pick (one tier below the Model Y's "+", owing to an Acceptable result on the updated moderate-overlap front test). NHTSA's new ADAS evaluation is a pass/fail check of basic driver-assist features — not Full Self-Driving — and doesn't change the crash-test star rating.

Two engineering reasons it tests so well:

A floor full of battery. The heavy pack sits low and flat, giving an exceptionally low center of gravity — among the lowest rollover risk of any SUV NHTSA has measured (about 7.9% in the original Model Y's testing). With no engine block up front, there's a large crumple zone, and the roof earns an IIHS "Good" rating — its standard requires the roof to withstand at least 4× the vehicle's weight.

Cameras that brace you for impact. A 2025 over-the-air update (software 2025.32.3) lets Tesla's Vision system anticipate some frontal collisions up to ~70 milliseconds before impact — beginning to tension the seatbelts and ready the airbags before traditional crash sensors register the hit. Tesla presents this as a supplement to conventional sensors, not a replacement.

A note on superlatives: NHTSA doesn't rank cars beyond the 5-star tier and has objected to "safest car" marketing, so this page avoids that framing. The accurate statement is simply that the Model Y earns the top result from every program that tests it.

Cold-weather hardware

Winter is where many buyers worry about EVs, and where the Model Y's hardware quietly answers most of the concern:

🔥

Standard heat pump

Standard on every Model Y. It heats the cabin and battery far more efficiently than resistance heat, protecting range in the cold.

❄️

Available all-wheel drive

Dual-motor AWD meters torque to each axle independently and instantly — traction control faster than any mechanical system.

🔋

~80–86% range retention

In independent cold-weather testing, a preconditioned Model Y holds most of its rated range rather than collapsing in the cold.

📱

Preheat before departure

Glass and seats can be warmed remotely or on a schedule — no idling to defrost, no scraping, cabin warm before anyone gets in.

The low, central battery mass also aids stability on slick roads. (Camera-based driver assistance is degraded by snow, ice, and glare — per Tesla's own manual — so winter is a place the car's hardware excels while the driver stays in command.)

02 — Efficiency

Every place you park becomes a place to fuel

The useful question isn't "is charging cheaper than gas?" — it almost always is. It's how far gas would have to fall to even break even with charging at home. The Model Y uses about 28.8 kWh per 100 miles (EPA). Plug in real numbers below:

At Iowa's residential rate (~13–16¢/kWh, EIA), home charging runs about 4–5¢ per mile. Against a typical 25–30 MPG gas SUV, gasoline would have to drop to roughly $1.00–$1.55 a gallon to compete — a price not seen in over a decade. Even against a 40–50 MPG hybrid, break-even only rises to about $1.90. At any realistic 2026 pump price, home charging wins comfortably.

The time and attention it gives back

Gasoline is a recurring errand — a few minutes, several times a month, always slightly off-route. Charging inverts it: plug in at home and the car is full every morning. A Tesla Wall Connector adds up to 44 miles of range per hour; even a standard 120V outlet adds ~3 mi/hr, which keeps pace with the average 37-mile day. Away from home, the 50,000+ chargers in Tesla's Destination network sit at hotels and restaurants — power gained while parked anyway.

Convenience that compounds

The phone is the key — the car unlocks on approach and locks when the driver walks away. There's no start button, no fob to manage, no warming up, no manual locking, and no planning charging stops for daily driving. Climate and battery can be preconditioned on a schedule, so the car is ready at departure time. The routine friction of car ownership mostly disappears.

Less to maintain, built to last

An electric drivetrain has a fraction of the moving parts. No oil changes, no spark plugs, no timing belts, no transmission service. Regenerative braking does most of the slowing with the motor, so pads and rotors last far longer. Consumer Reports' analysis of hundreds of thousands of owners found EV drivers pay about half as much to maintain and repair their cars — an average ~$4,600 saved over the life of the vehicle (CR, 2020).

The frunk

A sealed 4.1 cu ft front trunk sits entirely outside the cabin — ideal for groceries, muddy gear, or anything that shouldn't share air with the interior. It has a drain plug, so it can be hosed out or filled with ice and used as a cooler. No gas SUV offers anything like it.

Ventilated front seats, a 15-speaker audio system, a large central touchscreen, the full active-safety suite, and free over-the-air map updates all come standard on the mainstream (Premium and Performance) trims. 05 — Performance

Supercar acceleration, mainstream money

Instant electric torque makes even the everyday trims feel quick. The standout is the Model 3 Performance: Tesla claims 0–60 mph in 2.9 seconds, and Edmunds independently measured 3.0 seconds — matching the launch of six-figure sports cars, for $54,990.

The Supercharger network

The largest, most reliable fast-charging network — 52% of all US fast-charge ports, ranked #1 by J.D. Power with just a ~4% failed-session rate.

07 — The fair questions

Common objections, answered honestly

"Aren't the FSD safety stats cherry-picked?"
+

It helps to be precise about what Tesla actually counts. Its FSD safety report logs two kinds of events: "major" collisions, where an airbag or other non-reversible restraint deploys, and "minor" collisions — any other impact above roughly an 8 km/h change in speed. The headline "7× fewer collisions" counts both, so the common rebuttal that "Tesla only counts airbag deployments" doesn't actually apply to these figures.

The comparison still isn't perfectly clean — but the biases cut both ways. Against Tesla's case: assisted miles skew toward highways, the safest roads per mile, and Teslas are newer and better-equipped than the average car. In Tesla's favor: the US baseline of one crash per ~702,000 miles is built from police-reported crashes, and NHTSA's own research finds roughly 60% of property-damage crashes and 32% of injury crashes are never reported to police (Blincoe et al., 2023). Tesla's cars log even minor impacts automatically, so that human baseline almost certainly undercounts real-world crashes — which works to understate Tesla's edge.

Because those distortions partly cancel, the most trustworthy comparison is the same Tesla drivers with the system on vs. off — counted identically and split by road type: ~7× fewer collisions overall and ~5× fewer on non-highway roads. An independent Dutch regulator's required reporting points the same way (~3.5× fewer). And the passive crash-test ratings come from independent labs, so they don't depend on any of this.

"Won't the battery wear out and cost a fortune to replace?"
+

This is the most common EV fear, and the data is reassuring. Tesla's 2026 Impact Report shows Model 3 and Model Y Long Range packs retaining about 85% of capacity after 200,000 miles, with degradation front-loaded then flattening — many packs are projected to pass 300,000 miles before dropping to 70%. Most drivers will replace the car before the battery becomes the reason to. Packs are also covered by an 8-year warranty.

"Isn't an electric car just a coal-powered car?"
+

Even on today's mixed grid, an EV converts far more of its energy into motion than a gas car, so it comes out ahead well-to-wheel — and the grid keeps getting cleaner each year, so the car effectively gets greener as it ages. The sister explainer, Power Shift: The Efficiency Revolution, walks through that math.

"Don't Teslas depreciate badly?"
+

Recent history isn't flattering: when Tesla cut new-car prices in 2023–2024, used values fell with them, and EVs as a class have depreciated faster than gas cars lately. The narrow, fair truth is that among EVs, the Model 3 holds value better than most rivals (~45% after five years). The savings live in fuel and maintenance, not the resale check — worth going in with eyes open.

"Is 'Full Self-Driving' actually self-driving?"
+

No — and it shouldn't be bought expecting that. Despite the name, FSD (Supervised) is a Level 2 assistance system that needs hands ready and eyes on the road, and as of 2026 it's under active federal review for how it behaves in poor visibility. Treat it as advanced assistance, not a chauffeur. The free standard Autopilot and active-safety features are the part most owners rely on daily.

"Aren't they just too expensive?"
+

The upfront price is a real barrier, and with the federal credit gone in 2026 there's no sticker discount. But total cost of ownership tells a different story: home charging that beats gas at any realistic pump price, roughly half the maintenance cost, and a feature set that pushes a comparably-equipped rival into the same price range. The savings are back-loaded into the years of ownership rather than the day of purchase.
