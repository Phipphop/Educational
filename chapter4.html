The Missing Battery

The US already generates enough electricity. The problem is that we throw most of the capacity away — and a battery is all it takes to stop.

↓ scroll to find out

Built for the Worst Hour of the Year

Every power grid is designed around a single moment: peak demand. In most of the US, that's a hot summer afternoon when air conditioners run simultaneously across an entire region. For a few hours a year, the grid has to deliver more electricity than at any other time.

So we built enough generation capacity for that moment. About 1,189 gigawatts of it — enough to power every home, business, and factory at full tilt simultaneously, with margin to spare.

The problem: the rest of the time, we don't need it. The average US power plant runs at about 40% of its capacity. We built a grid for 100% and use 40% on average — and then spend billions maintaining the other 60% in standby, just in case.

Two Kinds of Power Plants

To understand what batteries change, you need to know how the grid currently handles variation in demand.

Baseload Plants

Nuclear, coal, large hydro, and combined-cycle gas plants run continuously. They're efficient when they run at their designed output — but expensive and slow to ramp up or down. They provide the steady foundation of the grid.

~56–93%

capacity factor — these plants run most of the time

Peaker Plants

Simple-cycle gas turbines that can spin up quickly to cover demand spikes. They're fast but inefficient — they burn more fuel per unit of electricity and cost more per MWh. They exist purely to handle the peaks that occur a few hours a year.

5–15%

capacity factor — peakers sit idle roughly 90% of the time

The result: we have two tiers of generation. Efficient plants that run constantly, and inefficient standby plants that cost billions to maintain but only run a few hundred hours per year. And when peakers do run, they produce expensive, carbon-heavy electricity to meet demand spikes that last just hours.

What Batteries Actually Do

A grid-scale battery doesn't generate electricity. It time-shifts it. Charge it at 3am when demand is low and plants have surplus capacity — then discharge it at 6pm when demand peaks and peakers would otherwise fire up.

This changes the economics of every other plant on the grid:

⚙️

Baseload plants run more efficiently

Power plants are most efficient at their design output. Constant-ramp operation wastes fuel. With batteries absorbing overnight surplus and covering evening peaks, baseload plants can run at a steadier, more optimal level — producing more electricity from the same fuel.

💰

Peaker plants become optional

Peakers cost $110–$228/MWh when running, plus capital and maintenance costs for sitting idle 90% of the time. A 4-hour battery can replace a peaker at equivalent or lower cost — and it produces zero emissions, zero fuel cost, and can respond in milliseconds rather than minutes.

⚡

Renewable curtailment stops

Wind and solar often generate more than the grid can use at that moment — so operators "curtail" it (switch it off). California alone curtailed over 2.4 million MWh of solar in 2022. Batteries absorb that surplus instead of wasting it, raising renewable utilization from ~23% (solar capacity factor) toward its actual potential.

🔒

Grid stability improves

Batteries respond to grid fluctuations in milliseconds — far faster than any spinning generator. They can provide frequency regulation, voltage support, and emergency backup capacity far more responsively than the fossil plants they replace. In some markets, this "ancillary service" revenue alone covers a significant fraction of battery costs.

The reframe

We don't need to build more generation to handle EVs and electrification. We need to use what we already built more intelligently. Batteries are the tool that turns a 40%-utilized grid into one that serves growing demand without new fossil fuel plants.

Already happening

California has retired or converted dozens of peaker plants since 2021, replacing them with battery storage. Arizona, Texas, and New England are following. This isn't a future projection — utilities are making this choice right now based on current economics.

EVs: Charging Is a Feature, Not a Bug

A fully electrified US vehicle fleet would represent approximately 14,000 GWh of distributed battery storage — more than a full day of US electricity consumption. You'd never draw all of it at once, but even 10–20% participation in smart charging programs would represent the largest flexible demand resource in history.

EVs that charge at night consume off-peak surplus that currently goes to waste — helping utilities run baseload plants more efficiently. And bidirectional charging (vehicle-to-grid, or V2G) allows EVs to sell stored electricity back to the grid during peak hours — turning millions of parked cars into a virtual power plant.

Grid operators already see this

California, Texas, and several New England states now offer EV charging incentives specifically timed to off-peak hours. Utilities don't worry about EVs crashing the grid at night — they worry about EV owners charging at 5pm. Smart charging infrastructure is the solution, and it's already being deployed.

Common Objections

"Batteries only store a few hours of power — what about week-long cloudy, calm weather?"
+

This is the right question to ask, and it's worth being precise about it. The batteries described in this chapter are solving a daily problem: peak demand that occurs for a few hours each day. Four-hour batteries are already cost-competitive and being deployed at scale for this purpose. They're not designed to cover multi-day weather events.

The multi-day problem — what grid engineers call "long-duration storage" — is real and actively being worked on. Solutions include pumped hydro (already the largest grid storage in the US at ~550 GWh), hydrogen from renewable electrolysis, grid-scale flow batteries, and geographic diversification (wind is usually blowing somewhere). None of these require a breakthrough — they need continued investment and deployment. The daily storage problem, which drives most of the economic case in this chapter, is already solved.

"Doesn't manufacturing batteries have a large environmental footprint?"
+

It does, and it's worth quantifying rather than dismissing. Lithium-ion battery manufacturing is energy and material intensive. A typical utility-scale battery system takes roughly 1–2 years of operation to "pay back" the carbon cost of its manufacture on the average US grid — similar to solar panels. Over a 15–20 year lifespan, the net benefit is substantial.

The more important comparison is against the alternative: peaker plants emit CO₂ every time they run, have their own construction footprint, and consume natural gas that requires the extraction and supply chain costs outlined in Chapter 3. Battery manufacturing is a one-time upfront cost; fossil generation is a recurring one.

"If batteries are so good, why isn't the grid already full of them?"
+

It's getting there — rapidly. US grid-scale battery capacity grew by 82% in 2023 alone, from ~11 GW to ~20 GW. Deployments are accelerating each year. The IRA tax credits reduced battery costs significantly and triggered a wave of projects.

The lag between "economically viable" and "fully deployed" is normal for major infrastructure. It took decades after natural gas became the cheapest option before it dominated the grid. Battery economics crossed the peaker-parity threshold around 2021–2022; we're now in the ramp-up phase. Regulatory frameworks, interconnection queues, and long-term utility planning cycles create inertia — but not fundamental barriers. The projects are being approved and built.

"What happens when a million EV owners all plug in at 6pm after work?"
+

This is the legitimate concern behind the "grid can't handle it" argument, and it's real — if charging happens in an uncoordinated, unmanaged way at peak hours, it adds demand precisely when the grid is most stressed.

The solution is smart charging: chargers that communicate with the grid and shift charging to off-peak hours, incentivized by time-of-use electricity pricing. Most EV owners charge overnight anyway — the car sits in a driveway for 10 hours and needs 8 kWh on average per day. Smart chargers can delay that charge until 2am and have it done before morning. Utilities actively want this demand — it lets them run baseload plants at higher utilization through the night rather than throttling down. The problem is coordination, not capacity.
