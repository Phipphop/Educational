Chapter 6

The Real Price Tag

You already know what you pay for energy. You probably don't know what energy actually costs — in health, in blackouts, in subsidies, and in disasters. The bill is already here. It's just hidden.

↓ scroll to find out

Two Different Bills

When people argue against electrification, the implicit assumption is that the current energy system is free — that transitioning means paying more for something we're getting for nothing. That assumption is wrong.

The current fossil fuel system carries enormous costs that don't show up on your energy bill. They show up in hospital bills, insurance premiums, FEMA disaster relief, federal tax subsidies, and the economic drag of blackouts. Americans are already paying them — collectively, every year — they just don't see the invoice.

Understanding the real cost of energy changes the question from "can we afford to transition?" to "can we afford not to?"

The Health Bill We're Already Paying

Burning fossil fuels releases fine particulate matter (PM2.5), nitrogen oxides, sulfur dioxide, and other pollutants directly into the air Americans breathe. The health consequences are large and well-documented.

A landmark 2021 study published in Environmental Research (Vohra et al., Harvard) found that fossil fuel combustion is responsible for 350,000 premature deaths in the United States every year — more than double previous estimates, because the study used updated risk models that account for health impacts at lower pollution concentrations than earlier research assumed.

Globally, the same study found 8.7 million deaths annually — approximately 1 in 5 deaths worldwide attributable to air pollution from burning fossil fuels.

The Reliability Bill

The US electrical grid loses more power per customer than almost any other developed country's grid. The American Society of Civil Engineers gives it a C– grade. Aging infrastructure, extreme weather events (which are increasing in frequency and severity), and underinvestment combine to produce a remarkably unreliable system for the world's largest economy.

The grid is getting less reliable, not more

Major power outages lasting more than an hour have increased significantly over the past two decades — primarily driven by more frequent and intense extreme weather events. The grid was designed for a climate that no longer exists. Modernization, which includes storage (Chapter 4) and distributed solar (Chapter 5), is the path to a more resilient system — not a more vulnerable one.

The Subsidy Irony

The fossil fuel industry receives significant direct subsidies from the federal government — tax breaks that exist specifically because of the political history of the oil and gas industry, not because they're economically justified.

These include the intangible drilling cost deduction (allowing oil companies to deduct ~70% of drilling costs in the first year), the percentage depletion allowance (allowing companies to deduct a fixed percentage of revenue from producing wells regardless of actual costs), and various other provisions that reduce the effective tax rate for fossil fuel companies well below the standard corporate rate.

The implicit subsidy argument

The IMF calculates global fossil fuel subsidies at $5.9 trillion per year when "implicit subsidies" are included — meaning the health, environmental, and climate costs that are imposed on the public but not reflected in the price of fuel. When fossil fuel companies sell gasoline at $3.50 a gallon, society absorbs the health and climate costs separately. The full cost of a gallon of gas — if it had to include its health and climate externalities — would be substantially higher. Renewable energy, which produces neither PM2.5 nor CO₂ at the point of generation, doesn't create this hidden tab.

The Moment Renewables Won

For decades, the argument against renewable energy was simple: it's too expensive. That argument is now factually reversed. New solar and wind are the cheapest sources of new electricity in most of the world — not just on environmental grounds, but on pure economics.

This is unsubsidized

These solar and wind costs are without the IRA tax credits that now make clean energy even cheaper. The subsidies exist to accelerate deployment — but solar and wind would be winning on economics alone in most markets even without them. The argument that renewables only work with subsidies was settled around 2018 and has become more wrong with every passing year.

The Case in Six Arguments

These chapters have built a single connected argument. Here it is in summary:

Chapter 1 — The Energy Illusion
66% wasted before it's used

The scale of the transition is overstated

Primary energy accounting includes the heat wasted in combustion. Renewables deliver the same useful energy from ~40 quads instead of 93.6 — a 57% reduction in total energy needed before we've done anything else.

Chapter 2 — The Efficiency Revolution
5× more efficient at the wheel

Electrification reduces demand, it doesn't add to it

An EV delivers 82% of electricity to the wheels; a gas car delivers 20% of fuel. Heat pumps deliver 2–4× more heat than gas furnaces per unit of energy. The grid can handle electrification because the energy demand per service goes down.

Chapter 3 — The Invisible Cost of Fuel
Only 11–16% reaches your wheels

The supply chain waste makes fossil fuels far worse than they appear

Well-to-wheel, gasoline converts only 11–16% of the original barrel's energy into motion. EVs on renewables convert 68–76%. The efficiency gap is 4–7× when the full chain is counted.

Chapter 4 — The Missing Battery
Grid runs at 40% capacity

We already have enough generation — we just waste most of it

Batteries let existing plants run at optimal efficiency, eliminate expensive peakers, absorb curtailed renewables, and are now cost-competitive. The grid can handle growing demand without new fossil plants.

Chapter 5 — Sunshine on Everything
~50% of demand from existing surfaces

Solar doesn't need new land

Rooftops, parking lots, farmland, and irrigation canals could supply roughly half of US electricity without displacing a single acre. Rooftop solar alone — NREL's figure — covers 34% of demand.

Chapter 6 — The Real Price Tag
$1T+ in hidden annual costs

The current system isn't free — it's just hidden

$820B in health costs, $150B in blackouts, $20B+ in direct subsidies, and growing climate disaster costs are already being paid. Electrification replaces this tab with systems that are now cheaper on raw economics alone.

Common Objections

"The $820B health cost figure sounds like it came from an advocacy group."
+

The synthesis report is from NRDC, which is an advocacy organization. That's worth flagging. However, the underlying 350,000 deaths figure comes from a peer-reviewed paper in Environmental Research (Vohra et al. 2021, Harvard), which is independent academic research. The economic translation of those deaths into dollar figures uses standard EPA methodology (Value of Statistical Life, or VSL) — the same method the EPA uses in its own regulatory cost-benefit analyses.

The 350,000 deaths figure is itself considered by some researchers to be conservative, as it uses risk estimates at PM2.5 concentrations where earlier epidemiological data is sparse. The direction of uncertainty is that actual mortality may be higher, not lower. We present the full range of confidence here rather than cite only the number that sounds biggest.

"If renewables are cheaper, why isn't the market already building them everywhere?"
+

It is — at a record pace. The US added more renewable generation capacity in 2023 than in any previous year, and the same is true globally. The IEA reported that clean energy investment globally surpassed fossil fuel investment for the first time in 2023.

The lag has several explanations: long-term utility contracts with existing fossil fuel plants that haven't expired, permitting and interconnection backlogs that can delay projects by years, political opposition in regions with fossil fuel economic interests, and the fact that economic crossover for solar/wind only happened around 2018–2020 — the market is responding but infrastructure takes time. The trend line is unambiguous: new fossil fuel investment is declining; new clean energy investment is at all-time highs and accelerating.

"Won't electrification cost trillions in upfront investment? Who pays for that?"
+

Yes, the transition requires significant capital investment — estimated in the range of $3–5 trillion over the next 20–30 years in the US to fully decarbonize electricity and transportation. That sounds enormous until you compare it to what we're already spending.

The US spends approximately $1.4 trillion per year on fossil fuels — oil, gas, and coal for transportation, heating, and power generation combined. Over 20 years, that's $28 trillion of ongoing expenditure on a system that also carries $1 trillion+ per year in hidden costs. The transition investment is roughly equivalent to two to three years of what we're already spending on fossil fuels, after which the ongoing costs are dramatically lower — fuel-free generation, fewer peakers, lower health costs. The question isn't whether we can afford to transition. It's whether we can afford to keep spending $28 trillion on the system we have.

"What about the jobs that would be lost in fossil fuel industries?"
+

This is a genuinely important concern that deserves a serious answer rather than dismissal. The fossil fuel industry directly employs roughly 1 million Americans, many in communities where there are few alternative employers. These jobs are often high-paying and represent real economic security for real people.

The clean energy sector already employs approximately 3.3 million Americans and is the fastest-growing job category in the country. Solar installer is the fastest-growing occupation in the Bureau of Labor Statistics data. The net job picture is strongly positive — but job creation in Phoenix doesn't help a coal miner in West Virginia whose mine closes. This is a just-transition problem that requires deliberate policy: retraining programs, investment in affected communities, prioritizing clean energy development in fossil fuel regions. The IRA explicitly includes provisions for this. The transition is winnable on jobs — but only if the displacement is treated as a serious obligation, not an afterthought.

The Question Was Never "Can We Afford It?"

The technologies exist. Solar, wind, batteries, heat pumps, and electric vehicles are all deployed at scale today — not as experiments, but as commercial products that are already cheaper than the alternatives they're replacing.

The transition doesn't require sacrificing comfort, convenience, or economic growth. It requires redirecting investment from a system that wastes two-thirds of its energy and generates $1 trillion per year in hidden costs, toward a system that is more efficient, more reliable, and increasingly cheaper.

The question was never whether we could afford to transition. It's whether we can afford to wait.
